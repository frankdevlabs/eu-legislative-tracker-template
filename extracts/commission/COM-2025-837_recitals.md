# Extract — COM(2025) 837, key recitals (curated subset)

> **Source:** Commission proposal **COM(2025) 837 final**, 19 November 2025 — *Digital Omnibus*.
> File 2025/0360 (COD). **Working transcription, not an official text.**
> **This is a curated subset** of the preamble (the recitals that carry the substantive choices tracked in
> this repo), not the full **60-recital** preamble. Verify against the authoritative document (EUR-Lex
> **CELEX 52025PC0837**). See [`../../NOTICE`](../../NOTICE).

Recitals are interpretive, not operative. They are reproduced here in their original proposal numbering so
they diff against [`../council/ST-9547-2026_recitals.md`](../council/ST-9547-2026_recitals.md) (where the

<a id="recital-27-personal-data"></a>
## Recital 27 — concept of personal data
Develops the relative/controller-centric reading behind the **Article 4(1)** addition: identifiability is
assessed by the means *"reasonably likely to be used"* by the entity in question; *"information is not to be
considered personal data for a given entity where that entity does not have means reasonably likely to be
used to identify the natural person"*; onward transmission to a party with such means renders the data
personal *only for that party*. Cites the CJEU case-law on the definition of personal data and tasks the
**Commission, together with the EDPB,** to stipulate technical criteria in an implementing act.
▸ This is the recital backing the contested Art 4(1) clause — see
[GDPR extract, point 1](COM-2025-837_gdpr-art3-amendments.md#point-1--article-4-definitions). The Council
kept a recital pointing back to the existing Recital 26 but **deleted the operative clause**.

## Recital 28 — scientific research
Elements for assessing whether research meets the scientific-research definition (methodological/systematic
approach; reliability, honesty, respect and accountability), in academic, industry and other settings
(Art 179(2) TFEU).

## Recital 29 — further processing for research/archiving/statistics
Reiterates that further processing for archiving in the public interest, scientific or historical research,
or statistical purposes is to be considered compatible lawful processing, without an Art 6(4) compatibility
test.

<a id="recital-30-ai-legitimate-interest"></a>
## Recital 30 — AI development and legitimate interest
Trustworthy AI relies on data, including personal data, across the AI life cycle; *"the processing of
personal data in this context may … be carried out for purposes of a legitimate interest within the meaning
of Article 6 …, where appropriate"*, without prejudice to other Union/national law and the other Art 6(1)(f)
conditions.
▸ This recital underpins the proposal's **Article 88c**. The Council **deleted recital 30** (and 31) and the
operative Art 88c, keeping only a reworked **recital 33a** — see
[council recitals](../council/ST-9547-2026_recitals.md#recital-33a-ai-legitimate-interest).

## Recital 31 — the AI balancing test
Guidance on balancing the controller's legitimate interest against the data subject's interests/rights
(benefit to the data subject and society, e.g. bias detection; reasonable expectations; safeguards such as
enhanced transparency, an unconditional right to object, privacy-preserving techniques, measures against
regurgitation and data leakage).
▸ **Deleted by the Council.**

## Recital 32 — scientific research as a legitimate interest
Processing for scientific research pursues a legitimate interest within the meaning of Art 6(1)(f), provided
the research is not contrary to Union or Member State law; balances Art 8 CFREU (data protection) with
Art 13 CFREU (freedom of science).

## Recital 33 — special-category data in AI development
Special categories may residually exist in training/testing/validation datasets or be retained in the AI
system/model though not necessary for the purpose; a derogation from Art 9(2) is allowed where the
controller has implemented effective technical/organisational measures and removes such data on
identification (or, if removal needs disproportionate effort, protects it from being used to infer outputs,
disclosed or made available). Underpins **Article 9(2)(k)/(5)**.

## Recital 34 — biometric verification
Distinguishes biometric **identification** ("one-to-many") from **verification/authentication**
("one-to-one"); allows a derogation where verification is necessary and the data subject has sole control of
the verification process. Underpins **Article 9(2)(l)**.

---

## Recital 35 — abuse of the right of access (Article 12)
The right of access should not be abused for purposes other than data protection (e.g. to manufacture a
refusal then claim compensation); controllers bear a **lower burden of proof** for the *excessive* character
of a request than for the *manifestly unfounded* character. Underpins **Article 12(5)**.

## Recital 36 — information-obligation exemption (Article 13(4))
Extends the Art 13(4) derogation to non-data-intensive, non-complex, low-risk relationships (e.g. a
craftsman and clients; associations and sport clubs) where the data subject can reasonably be expected to
already have the information.

## Recital 37 — research exemption from information duties (Article 13(5))
Where processing is for scientific research and providing the information is impossible or a disproportionate
effort, the controller may inform indirectly (e.g. by making the information publicly available).

## Recital 38 — automated decisions (Article 22)
Clarifies that decisions based solely on automated processing are allowed where the Art 22 conditions are
met, and that for the contractual-necessity ground *"it should not be required that the decision could be
taken only by solely automated processing"* — i.e. the fact a human could also take it does not bar solely
automated processing; where several equally effective solutions exist, the less intrusive should be used.
▸ This recital backs the contested Art 22 rewrite. The Council **deleted recital 38** and largely restored
Art 22 — see [GDPR extract, point 7](COM-2025-837_gdpr-art3-amendments.md#point-7--article-22-automated-decisions).

## Recital 39 — breach-notification threshold (Article 33)
Aligns the Art 33 notification threshold with the Art 34 communication threshold (**high-risk only**); the
EDPB prepares a common template and a high-risk-circumstances list, adopted by the Commission; reviewed at
least every three years.

## Recital 40 — DPIA lists (Article 35)
A **single EU-level** list of processing operations subject to (and exempt from) a DPIA replaces the national
lists; mandatory publication of the no-DPIA list; lists/template/methodology prepared by the EDPB and adopted
by the Commission.

---

## Recitals 41–43 — EUDPR / Directive (EU) 2016/680 alignment
Regulation (EU) 2018/1725 (EU institutions) and Directive (EU) 2016/680 are to be brought into alignment with
the GDPR amendments; provisions of the EUDPR following the same principles as the GDPR are to be interpreted
homogeneously. Underpins [Article 4](COM-2025-837_eudpr-art4.md).

## Recitals 44–48 — terminal-equipment processing & ePrivacy
Storing/accessing personal data in terminal equipment, and subsequent processing, should be governed by a
**single framework — the GDPR** — where the user is a natural person (recital 44), on the basis of **consent**
with a limitative list of consent-exempt purposes (recital 44); a **no-repeat** rule after a refused consent
(recital 45); **automated/machine-readable** choice signals, with a **media-service-provider** carve-out and
a browser technical-means duty (recital 46); ePrivacy Art 5(3) remains only for non-natural-person subscribers
and non-personal information (recital 47); **ePrivacy Article 4 is repealed** as overlapping with NIS2 and the
GDPR (recital 48).
▸ These recitals back **GDPR Articles 88a/88b** and the **ePrivacy** carve-out; the Council reversed the
design (cookies stay in ePrivacy) — see [council recitals 39/39a](../council/ST-9547-2026_recitals.md) and
[ePrivacy extract](COM-2025-837_eprivacy-art5.md).

## Recitals 49–57 — single-entry point for incident reporting
Establish the **single-entry point** (recital 49) on **ENISA**, its security (recital 50), piloting and
Commission assessment before go-live (recital 51), continuity with national technical solutions (recital 52),
information types/formats (recital 53), alignment with DORA's reporting templates (recital 54), inter-authority
information exchange (recital 55), the acts amended and the 18-/24-month timing (recital 56), and alternative
means on technical impossibility (recital 57). Underpins [Articles 6–9](COM-2025-837_cyber-art6-9.md).

## Recital 58 — EDPS / EDPB consultation
The EDPS was consulted under Art 42(1) and the EDPB under Art 42(2) of Regulation (EU) 2018/1725; opinions
*[DATE]* (placeholders in the source).

## Recital 59 — repeal of the P2B Regulation
Regulation (EU) 2019/1150 (P2B) should be **repealed**, its objectives largely covered by the DSA and DMA;
selected definitions (Art 2), restriction/suspension rules (Art 4), the internal complaint-handling system
(Art 11) and enforcement (Art 15) **temporarily remain** until the cross-referencing acts are amended.
Underpins [Article 10](COM-2025-837_final-art10-11.md#article-10-amendments-repeals-transitory).

## Recital 60 — immediate entry into force
Given the technical nature and urgency, the Regulation should enter into force immediately after publication,
with transitional periods as appropriate.
