<a id="point-1--article-4-definitions"></a>
## Point 1 — Article 4 (definitions)

**(a) Addition to Art 4(1) (concept of personal data).** In point 1, the following sentences are added:
*"Information relating to a natural person is not necessarily personal data for every other person or
entity, merely because another entity can identify that natural person. Information shall not be personal
for a given entity where that entity cannot identify the natural person to whom the information relates,
taking into account the means reasonably likely to be used by that entity. Such information does not become
personal for that entity merely because a potential subsequent recipient has means reasonably likely to be
used to identify the natural person to whom the information relates."*
▸ The relative / controller-centric clarification of "personal data" — the single most contested change in
the proposal. **The Council later deleted this** ([council point 1](../council/ST-9547-2026_gdpr-art3-amendments.md#point-1--article-4-definitions)).
See provision page.

**(b) New definitions added to Article 4:**

- **(32) 'terminal equipment'** = terminal equipment as set out in Article 1(1) of Directive 2008/63/EC;
- **(33) 'electronic communications networks'** — the definition of Article 2(1) of Directive (EU) 2018/1972
  shall apply;
- **(34) 'web browser'** = web browser as defined in Article 2(11) of Regulation (EU) 2022/1925;
- **(35) 'media service'** = a media service as defined in Article 2(1) of Regulation (EU) 2024/1083;
- **(36) 'media service provider'** = a media service provider as defined in Article 2(2) of Regulation (EU)
  2024/1083;
- **(37) 'online interface'** = an online interface as defined in Article 3(m) of Regulation (EU) 2022/2065;
- **(38) 'scientific research'** = *"any research which can also support innovation, such as technological
  development and demonstration. These actions shall contribute to existing scientific knowledge or apply
  existing knowledge in novel ways, be carried out with the aim of contributing to the growth of society's
  general knowledge and wellbeing and adhere to ethical standards in the relevant research area. This does
  not exclude that the research may also aim to further a commercial interest."*
  ▸ The proposal's scientific-research definition explicitly includes the *"may also aim to further a
  commercial interest"* sentence; the Council **reworked** it (and dropped that explicit sentence).

## Point 2 — Article 5(1)(b) (purpose limitation)

Replaced by: *"collected for specified, explicit and legitimate purposes and not further processed in a
manner that is incompatible with those purposes; further processing for archiving purposes in the public
interest, scientific or historical research purposes or statistical purposes shall, in accordance with
Article 89(1), be considered to be compatible with the initial purposes, independent of the conditions of
Article 6(4) of this Regulation, ('purpose limitation');"*

<a id="point-3--article-9-special-categories"></a>
## Point 3 — Article 9 (special categories of personal data)

**(a) New points added to Article 9(2):**

- **(k)** processing in the context of the **development and operation of an AI system** as defined in
  Article 3, point (1), of Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 or an AI model, subject to the conditions referred to
  in paragraph 5.
- **(l)** processing of **biometric data** is necessary for the purpose of confirming the identity of a data
  subject (**verification**), where the biometric data or the means needed for the verification is under the
  **sole control of the data subject**.

**(b) New Article 9(5):**
*"For processing referred to in point (k) of paragraph 2, appropriate organisational and technical measures
shall be implemented to avoid the collection and otherwise processing of special categories of personal
data. Where, despite the implementation of such measures, the controller identifies special categories of
personal data in the datasets used for training, testing or validation or in the AI system or AI model, the
controller shall **remove** such data. If removal of those data requires disproportionate effort, the
controller shall in any event effectively protect without undue delay such data from being used to produce
outputs, from being disclosed or otherwise made available to third parties."*
▸ The proposal uses **"remove"**; the Council later changed this to **"erase"** and added a regular-
verification + documentation duty across the AI life cycle
([council point 3](../council/ST-9547-2026_gdpr-art3-amendments.md#point-3--article-9-special-categories)).
See Art 9 provision.

<a id="point-4--article-12-5-abusive-requests"></a>
## Point 4 — Article 12(5) (manifestly unfounded / excessive requests)

Paragraph 5 replaced by: *"Information provided under Articles 13 and 14 and any communication and any
actions taken under Articles 15 to 22 and 34 shall be provided free of charge. Where requests from a data
subject are manifestly unfounded or excessive, in particular because of their repetitive character or also,
for requests under Article 15 because the data subject **abuses the rights conferred by this regulation for
purposes other than the protection of their data**, the controller may either: (a) charge a reasonable fee
taking into account the administrative costs…; or (b) refuse to act on the request. The controller shall
bear the burden of demonstrating that the request is manifestly unfounded or that there are reasonable
grounds to believe that it is excessive."*
▸ The proposal's "abuses the rights … for purposes other than the protection of their data" standard was
**reformulated by the Council** to an "abusive intention … can be demonstrated" test
([council point 4](../council/ST-9547-2026_gdpr-art3-amendments.md#point-4--article-12-5-abusive-requests)).
See Art 12/57 provision.

---

<a id="point-5--article-13-4-information-obligations"></a>
## Point 5 — Article 13(4) (information obligation exemption)

Replaced by: *"Paragraphs 1, 2 and 3 shall not apply where the personal data have been collected in the
context of a **clear and circumscribed relationship** between data subjects and a controller exercising an
activity that is **not data-intensive** and there are reasonable grounds to assume that the data subject
already has the information referred to in points (a) and (c) of paragraph 1, unless the controller
transmits the data to other recipients or categories of recipients, transfers the data to a third country,
carries out automated decision-making, including profiling, referred to in Article 22(1), or the processing
is likely to result in a high risk to the rights and freedoms of data subjects within the meaning of
Article 35."*

## Point 6 — Article 13(5) (research exemption, new paragraph)

Added: *"When the processing takes place for scientific research purposes and the provision of information
referred to under paragraphs 1, 2 and 3 proves impossible or would involve a disproportionate effort
subject to the conditions and safeguards referred to in Article 89(1) or in so far as the obligation
referred to in paragraph 1 of this Article is likely to render impossible or seriously impair the
achievement of the objectives of that processing, the controller does not need to provide the information
referred to under paragraphs 1, 2 and 3. In such cases the controller shall take appropriate measures to
protect the data subject's rights and freedoms and legitimate interests, including making the information
publicly available."*

<a id="point-7--article-22-automated-decisions"></a>
## Point 7 — Article 22 (automated individual decision-making)

Paragraphs 1 and 2 replaced by: *"1. A decision which produces legal effects for a data subject or
similarly significantly affects him or her may be based solely on automated processing, including
profiling, only where that decision: (a) is necessary for entering into, or performance of, a contract
between the data subject and a data controller **regardless of whether the decision could be taken
otherwise than by solely automated means**; (b) is authorised by Union or Member State law to which the
controller is subject and which also lays down suitable measures to safeguard the data subject's rights and
freedoms and legitimate interests; or (c) is based on the data subject's explicit consent."*
▸ The proposal **rewrites Article 22** into a permissive "may be based solely on automated processing only
where" form, and adds the **"regardless of whether the decision could be taken otherwise than by solely
automated means"** clause in (a). The Council later **struck that clause** and **largely restored** the
current Article 22 ([council point 7](../council/ST-9547-2026_gdpr-art3-amendments.md#point-7--article-22-automated-decisions)).
See Art 22 provision.

---

<a id="point-8--article-33-breach-notification"></a>
## Point 8 — Article 33 (personal-data-breach notification)

**(a) Article 33(1) replaced:** *"In the case of a personal data breach that is **likely to result in a
high risk** to the rights and freedoms of natural persons, the controller shall without undue delay and,
where feasible, not later than **96 hours** after having become aware of it, notify the personal data breach
**via the single-entry point established pursuant to Article 23a of Directive (EU) 2022/2555** to the
supervisory authority competent in accordance with Article 55 and Article 56. Where the notification to the
supervisory authority is not made within 96 hours, it shall be accompanied by reasons for the delay."*
▸ Threshold raised to **high-risk only** (was: any breach unless unlikely to risk); deadline set at **96
hours**; routed through the **single-entry point** (Art 23a NIS2). The Council kept this; note the Council
renamed the routing to a **national entry point (Art 23b)** and moved the switch-over to 30 months.
See [Art 33 provision](../../docs/provisions/gdpr-art33-breach-notification.md).

**(b) New Article 33(1a):** until the single-entry point exists, controllers continue to notify the
competent supervisory authority directly (Articles 55, 56).

**(c) New Article 33(6) and (7):** the **Board** (EDPB) shall prepare and transmit to the **Commission** a
proposal for a common template for breach notification and for a list of circumstances in which a breach is
likely to result in a high risk, within **[OP date = nine months of the entry into application]**; the
Commission may adopt it by implementing act (Art 93(2)); template and list reviewed at least every three
years.
▸ Under the proposal the Commission **adopts** the template/list by implementing act; the Council later moved
this power to the **EDPB** (establishes and makes public).

---

<a id="point-9--article-35-dpia-lists"></a>
## Point 9 — Article 35 (data protection impact assessment)

**(a) Paragraphs 4, 5, 6 replaced:** the **Board** shall prepare and transmit to the **Commission** a
proposal for (4) a list of the kind of processing operations subject to a DPIA, (5) a list of those for
which no DPIA is required, and (6) a common template and methodology for DPIAs.

**(b) New paragraphs 6a–6c:** the proposals to be submitted to the Commission within **[OP date = 9 months
of the entry into application]**; the **Commission** is empowered to adopt them by implementing act
(Art 93(2)); review at least every three years; existing national DPA lists remain valid until the
Commission adopts the implementing act.
▸ The proposal creates a **single EU-level** DPIA list but vests adoption in the **Commission**; the Council
later moved the power to the **EDPB**
([council point 9](../council/ST-9547-2026_gdpr-art3-amendments.md#point-9--article-35-dpia-lists)).

<a id="point-10--article-29a-pseudonymisation"></a>
## Point 10 — New Article 41a (pseudonymisation criteria — Commission implementing power)

The following article is added:
*"Article 41a — (1) The Commission may adopt implementing acts to specify means and criteria to determine
whether data resulting from pseudonymisation no longer constitutes personal data for certain entities.
(2) For the purpose of paragraph 1 the Commission shall: (a) assess the state of the art of available
techniques; (b) develop criteria and or categories for controllers and recipients to assess the risk of
re-identification in relation to typical recipients of data. (3) The implementation of the means and
criteria outlined in an implementing act may be used as an element to demonstrate that data cannot lead to
reidentification of the data subjects. (4) The Commission shall closely involve the EDPB in the preparations
of the implementing acts. The EDPB shall issue an opinion on the draft implementing acts within a deadline
of 8 weeks as of the receipt of the draft from the Commission. (5) The Implementing Acts shall be adopted in
accordance with the examination procedure referred to in Article 93(3)."*
▸ This is the proposal's **Article 41a** (Commission *implementing power*). The Council **replaced it** with
an EDPB-opinion mechanism renumbered to **Article 29a**
([council point 10](../council/ST-9547-2026_gdpr-art3-amendments.md#point-10--article-29a-pseudonymisation)).
The anchor here mirrors the Council slug so the two diff cleanly. See
Art 4 provision.

<a id="point-11--article-57-supervisory-authority-requests"></a>
## Point 11 — Article 57(1) (tasks of the supervisory authority)

**Point (k) is deleted.**
▸ Removes the SA's duty to provide information to data subjects free of charge in all cases. (The Council
also reworked Art 57(4) on abusive requests — not present as a separate point in the proposal.)
See Art 12/57 provision.

## Points 12–14 — Articles 64 & 70 (EDPB tasks and consistency)

- **12.** Article 64(1), point (a) is **deleted**.
- **13.** Article 70(1), point (h) is **deleted**.
- **14.** New points inserted in Article 70(1): **(ha)** prepare/transmit a proposal for the DPIA lists
  (Art 35); **(hb)** prepare/transmit a proposal for the DPIA common template and methodology (Art 35);
  **(hc)** prepare/transmit a proposal for the breach-notification common template and the high-risk-
  circumstances list (Art 33).

<a id="point-15--articles-88-machine-readable-consent"></a>
## Point 15 — New Articles 88a, 88b, 88c (after Article 88)

The proposal inserts **three** new articles after Article 88.

**Article 88a — Processing of personal data in the terminal equipment of natural persons.**
- (1) Storing of personal data, or gaining of access to personal data already stored, in a natural person's
  terminal equipment is allowed only with that person's **consent**, in accordance with this Regulation.
- (2) Paragraph 1 does not preclude storing/access based on Union or Member State law within the meaning of,
  and subject to the conditions of, Article 6, to safeguard the objectives in Article 23(1).
- (3) Without consent, storing/access and subsequent processing is lawful to the extent necessary for:
  (a) carrying out the transmission of an electronic communication over a network; (b) providing a service
  explicitly requested by the data subject; (c) **creating aggregated information about the usage of an
  online service to measure the audience** of such a service, carried out by the controller of that online
  service solely for its own use; (d) **maintaining or restoring the security** of a service requested by the
  data subject or the terminal equipment used for it.
- (4) Where based on consent: (a) the data subject must be able to **refuse** in an easy and intelligible
  manner with a **single-click button or equivalent means**; (b) after consent, no new request for the same
  purpose while consent can lawfully be relied on; (c) after a refusal, no new request for the same purpose
  for **at least six months**. This paragraph also applies to subsequent processing based on consent.
- (5) *This Article shall apply from [OP: please insert the date = 6 months following the date of entry into
  force of this Regulation].*

▸ Article 88a **moves the cookie / terminal-equipment consent rule out of the ePrivacy Directive and into
the GDPR**. The Council **deleted Article 88a** and kept the consent regime in an **amended ePrivacy
Directive** instead — see [`../council/ST-9547-2026_eprivacy-art5.md`](../council/ST-9547-2026_eprivacy-art5.md)
and cookies provision.

**Article 88b — Automated and machine-readable indications of data subject's choices.**
- (1) Controllers must ensure their **online interfaces** allow data subjects to: (a) give consent through
  automated and machine-readable means; (b) decline a request for consent and exercise the right to object
  (Art 21(2)) through automated and machine-readable means.
- (2) Controllers must **respect** those choices.
- (3) Paragraphs 1 and 2 **do not apply to media service providers** when providing a media service.
- (4) The Commission shall request European standardisation organisations (Art 10(1) of Reg (EU) 1025/2012)
  to draft standards; interfaces conforming to published harmonised standards are **presumed in conformity**.
- (5) Paragraphs 1 and 2 apply from *[OP: insert date = 24 months following entry into force]*.
- (6) **Providers of web browsers, which are not SMEs**, shall provide the technical means for data subjects
  to give/refuse consent and exercise the Art 21(2) objection through those automated machine-readable means.
- (7) Paragraph 6 applies from *[OP: insert date = 48 months following entry into force]*.

▸ Article 88b is the **machine-readable consent signal** article. The Council **retained** it (renumbered,
and extended the technical-means duty to operating-system providers)
([council point 15](../council/ST-9547-2026_gdpr-art3-amendments.md#point-15--articles-88-machine-readable-consent)).

**Article 88c — Processing in the context of the development and operation of AI.**
*"Where the processing of personal data is necessary for the interests of the controller in the context of
the development and operation of an AI system as defined in Article 3, point (1), of Regulation (EU)
2024/1689 or an AI model, such processing may be pursued for **legitimate interests within the meaning of
Article 6(1)(f)** of Regulation (EU) 2016/679, where appropriate, except where other Union or national laws
explicitly require consent, and where such interests are overridden by the interests, or fundamental rights
and freedoms of the data subject which require protection of personal data, in particular where the data
subject is a child. Any such processing shall be subject to appropriate organisational, technical measures
and safeguards … such as to ensure respect of data minimisation … to protect against non-disclosure of
residually retained data … to ensure enhanced transparency … and providing data subjects with an
unconditional right to object …"*
▸ Article 88c makes **AI development/operation a legitimate interest** under Art 6(1)(f). The Council
**deleted Article 88c**; the interpretive statement survives only in **recital 33a** — see
[`../council/ST-9547-2026_recitals.md`](../council/ST-9547-2026_recitals.md#recital-33a-ai-legitimate-interest)
and AI LI provision.
